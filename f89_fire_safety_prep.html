📖 Code & Terms Reference
1 · Fire Science Fundamentals
Fire TriangleThree things every fire needs: Heat + Fuel + Oxygen. Remove any one and the fire goes out — this is the basis of all extinguishing.
Fire TetrahedronAdds a fourth element to the triangle: the uninhibited Chemical Chain Reaction. Modern extinguishers (dry chem, halon-replacements) work by breaking this reaction.
Stages of FireIncipient (just ignited, small) → Growth (spreading) → Fully Developed (peak heat) → Decay (fuel running out). Sprinklers aim to catch fires in incipient/growth.
ConductionHeat transfer through direct contact — fire spreads through a steel beam to the next room. Why fire-rated assemblies matter.
ConvectionHeat transfer through moving air/smoke — hot gases rise into stairwells, shafts, ceilings. The dominant method of spread in high-rises.
RadiationHeat transfer through electromagnetic waves — fire ignites materials across a room without touching them. Why "exposure protection" matters.
FlashoverEntire room reaches ignition temperature at once — everything combustible bursts into flame simultaneously. Usually fatal to anyone inside.
BackdraftOxygen-starved fire explodes when a door/window suddenly admits air. Warning signs: dark smoke pulsing in/out, no visible flame, hot door.
Stack EffectHigh-rise chimney effect: warm interior air rises through stairs/shafts, pulling smoke up. Stronger in winter, makes upper floors smoke-fill fast.
CompartmentationBuilding divided into fire-rated compartments (walls, floors, doors) to contain a fire to its room of origin. The strategy that makes "defend in place" possible.
2 · Fire Protection Systems
Wet Pipe SprinklerPipes always filled with water. Most common. Heat melts the fusible link on a head → water discharges immediately from THAT head only.
Dry Pipe SprinklerPipes filled with pressurized air; water held back by a dry-pipe valve. Used in unheated areas (parking garages, attics) to prevent freezing.
Preaction SprinklerTwo-step: a detection signal opens the valve, THEN a sprinkler head must also fuse. Used where water damage must be avoided (data centers, museums).
Deluge SystemAll heads are OPEN. When a detector trips, water floods every head simultaneously. Used in high-hazard areas (aircraft hangars, transformer rooms).
OS&Y ValveOutside Stem & Yoke — the main sprinkler control valve. Stem visible OUT = valve OPEN. Stem IN = valve CLOSED. FSD checks this daily.
PIVPost Indicator Valve — outdoor sprinkler control valve with an "OPEN/SHUT" window. Same function as OS&Y, different form factor.
Water Flow SwitchDetects water moving in a sprinkler pipe and trips the alarm. Confirms an actual discharge, not just an electrical signal.
Tamper SwitchDetects a sprinkler control valve being closed and sends a Supervisory signal to the FACP. Prevents a closed valve from going unnoticed.
Standpipe Class IFor FDNY use only — 2.5" hose outlets. The standard in NYC office HRBs.
Standpipe Class IIFor occupant use — 1.5" hose with hose already attached. Largely phased out; occupants should evacuate, not fight fires.
Standpipe Class IIICombination — both 2.5" (FDNY) and 1.5" (occupant) outlets at each floor.
Siamese ConnectionThe dual-inlet fitting on the building exterior where FDNY pumpers boost water pressure into the standpipe/sprinkler system. Must stay unobstructed.
Annunciator PanelDisplay showing which zone/floor/device is in alarm. Found at the FCS and sometimes at building entrances for FDNY use.
Central StationOff-site monitoring service that receives the building's alarm signals and notifies FDNY. Required for most commercial fire alarm systems.
3 · FLSD Duties & Responsibilities
Daily InspectionFSD verifies fire alarm panel normal, sprinkler valves open (OS&Y stems out), exits clear, FCS equipment functional. Logged each shift.
LogbookBound, page-numbered record of all inspections, drills, impairments, alarms, and FDNY visits. Kept at the FCS. FDNY can demand to see it.
StaffingFSD or DFSD must be on premises during all business hours. Building cannot operate without coverage.
Fire DrillsMinimum 2 per year. FSD coordinates, Fire Wardens run their floors, results logged. Drills test both staff response AND occupant familiarity.
ImpairmentAny fire protection system out of service. FSD must immediately notify FDNY, building owner, and central station; institute a fire watch; log everything.
Fire WatchRequired when a system is impaired — trained person continuously patrols affected areas with a means of notifying occupants and calling FDNY.
EAP CoordinationFSD oversees the Emergency Action Plan — staff training, drills for non-fire emergencies (medical, bomb, active shooter, hazmat).
FDNY LiaisonOn FDNY arrival, FSD provides: alarm location, building systems status, occupant status, special hazards, Building Information Card (BIC).
4 · Emergency Action Plan (EAP)
Partial EvacuationFire floor + floor above relocated first. The default response in a modern HRB. Limits stairwell congestion, lets FDNY ascend.
Full EvacuationEveryone out. Ordered by FDNY Incident Commander, not by FSD. Used when conditions exceed the building's containment capacity.
RelocationMove occupants to a safe area in the SAME building — at least 2 floors below the fire. Not full evacuation to the street.
Shelter-in-PlaceOccupants stay where they are (or move to interior rooms). Used for active shooter, severe weather, exterior hazmat — when LEAVING is more dangerous.
Defend in PlaceFire-specific version of shelter — sprinklered, compartmented building means far-removed floors are safer staying put than crowding stairs.
ReentryAfter an all-clear from FDNY ONLY. FSD makes the announcement. Never on FSD's own initiative — even if smoke seems to clear.
Bomb ThreatNYPD-led response. FSD: notify 911 immediately, document the call (time/words/voice), follow NYPD direction on search vs. evacuation.
Active ShooterRun / Hide / Fight (DHS protocol). FSD assists NYPD with building info, announcements, lockdown systems. Do NOT pull the fire alarm.
Medical EmergencyCall 911, deploy AED if available, clear path for EMS, meet EMS at the lobby and escort to the patient.
Disabled OccupantsPre-identified in the EAP. Move to the Area of Refuge (typically a stair landing or rated lobby) and report location to FSD for FDNY pickup.
5 · FDNY Communications
Plain LanguageNo codes, no jargon. Say "fire on the 14th floor," not "Code Red Sector 4." Codes confuse occupants and arriving FDNY units.
Announcement Order(1) Attention tone (2) Identify location (3) Identify affected floors (4) Direct action — which stair, where to go (5) Reassure.
PA SystemOne-way only, from FCS to occupants. Used for evacuation/relocation/all-clear announcements to specific floors or building-wide.
Warden PhonesTwo-way phones between the FCS and each floor's Fire Warden station. The primary way FSD gets status reports from each floor.
Brief FDNY ICOn arrival give: location of alarm, type of device, action taken, occupant status, systems status, special hazards, BIC. Stay at FCS to assist.
Don't SpeculateReport what you KNOW. "Smoke detector activated on 14" — not "I think there's a fire on 14." Speculation triggers wrong responses.
6 · Building Construction & High-Rise
Fire-Rated WallWall rated to contain fire for a specified time (1-hr, 2-hr, 3-hr). Penetrations must be firestopped. Holes from cable installs = code violation.
Fire DoorSelf-closing, latching, rated door. Must NEVER be propped open. Common violation. Hold-opens must be magnetic and release on alarm.
Smokeproof TowerStairwell with a vestibule/balcony at each floor — smoke can't enter the stair without crossing open air. Used in some older HRBs.
Stair PressurizationFans pressurize the stairwell to keep smoke out. Activated automatically on alarm. Modern HRB equivalent of smokeproof tower.
Area of RefugeProtected location (rated stair landing or rated room) where disabled occupants wait for FDNY rescue. Usually has a two-way comm device.
Building Info CardBIC — quick-reference card with building systems, hazards, layout. Posted at FCS. Handed to FDNY IC on arrival.
Utility ShutoffsFSD must know location of: main gas shutoff, main water, main electric, fuel oil. May be directed to shut by FDNY.
Vertical SpreadFire/smoke spreads UP through shafts, stairs, plenums, poke-throughs. Why floor-above evacuation is critical.
7 · NYC Fire Code & Regulations
Occupancy: BBusiness — offices. The occupancy class F-89 covers. Daytime population, low fuel load, alert adult occupants.
Occupancy: AAssembly — 50+ people gathered (auditoriums, restaurants, conference centers). Higher exit and sprinkler requirements.
Occupancy: RResidential — hotels (R-1), apartments (R-2). Different CoF (T-89/F-89 hotel) applies; sleeping occupants change the strategy.
Drill Minimum2 fire drills per year minimum in office HRBs. Documented in the logbook, signed off by the FSD.
Exit MinimumAt least 2 independent exits from every floor. Maximum travel distance to an exit is code-limited (typically 200 ft sprinklered).
Impairment NoticeAny fire system impairment must be reported IMMEDIATELY — to FDNY, central station, and building owner. Fire watch must begin.
Record RetentionLogbook entries, drill records, inspection reports kept on premises and available to FDNY. Typical retention is 3 years.
FSP RequiredEvery office HRB must have a Comprehensive Fire Safety and EAP on file with FDNY. FSD enforces and implements it.
8 · On-Site Priority Order
Step 1 · VerifyConfirm the alarm on the FACP. Note zone, floor, device type. If possible, send a Fire Warden or building staff to visually verify.
Step 2 · NotifyCall 911. Never rely on automatic transmission alone. Confirm FDNY is en route. Then notify building owner/management.
Step 3 · AnnouncePA announcement to fire floor and floor above: identify location, direct to safe stair, instruct relocation. Plain language.
Step 4 · CoordinateMaintain comms with Fire Wardens via warden phones. Receive status from each affected floor. Track relocated/missing occupants.
Step 5 · Brief FDNYOn arrival, hand off to IC: alarm location, action taken, occupant status, systems status, BIC, special hazards. Remain at FCS.
Step 6 · DocumentLog everything in the logbook — time of alarm, actions taken, communications, FDNY arrival, all-clear. Required record.
Never Skip Step 1Don't announce, don't evacuate, don't speculate before verifying. False alarms are common; wrong action causes injury.
9 · Fire Command Center Operations
Alarm SignalHighest priority. Indicates a fire device (smoke detector, heat detector, pull station, water flow) has activated. Triggers full response.
Supervisory SignalA condition that affects fire protection (closed valve, low water, pump trouble). NOT a fire — but a system is compromised. Investigate immediately.
Trouble SignalAn electrical fault in the alarm system itself — broken wire, ground fault, low battery. System may not work properly. Log and call service.
AcknowledgeSilences the panel buzzer but does NOT clear the alarm. Tells the panel "I see it." The alarm condition remains active.
ResetClears an alarm AFTER the condition has been resolved and verified. Only done when safe — and often only with FDNY approval.
SilenceQuiets audible notification appliances (horns, bells) once occupants have been alerted. Visual alarm continues.
Signal PriorityAlarm > Supervisory > Trouble. If multiple signals are active, ALARM is always handled first.
10 · Human Behavior in Emergencies
Denial PhaseMost occupants' first reaction is to disbelieve the alarm. They finish emails, gather belongings, hesitate. Clear, calm announcements override denial.
Crowd FlowPeople follow the leader and the crowd — not the signs. Trained Fire Wardens visibly directing the flow are more effective than any sign.
BottlenecksForm at stair doors, lobby exits, and turns. Fire Wardens keep people moving, prevent stopping to look back, manage pace.
Panic Is RareTrue panic is uncommon — most people are slow, not panicked. The real problem is delayed start, not chaos. Speed up the start.
Calm VoiceFSD announcements should be slow, clear, low-pitch, repeated. Urgency in the words; calm in the delivery. Panicked voice creates panic.
Familiar RoutesPeople instinctively return to the way they came in — even if it's blocked. Drills train them to use the nearest exit instead.
