📖 Code & Terms Reference
1 · Fire Science Fundamentals
Fire TriangleThree things every fire needs: Heat + Fuel + Oxygen. Remove any one and the fire goes out — this is the basis of all extinguishing.
Fire TetrahedronAdds a fourth element to the triangle: the uninhibited Chemical Chain Reaction. Modern extinguishers (dry chem, halon-replacements) work by breaking this reaction.
Stages of FireIncipient (just ignited — an extinguisher can control it) → Growth (rollover stage) → Fully Developed (flashover stage, peak temps) → Decay/Burnout (backdraft risk).
ConductionHeat transfer through direct contact — fire spreads through a steel beam to the next room. Why fire-rated assemblies matter.
ConvectionHeat transfer through moving air/smoke — hot gases rise into stairwells, shafts, ceilings. The dominant method of spread in high-rises.
RadiationHeat transfer through electromagnetic waves — fire ignites materials across a room without touching them. Why "exposure protection" matters.
RolloverIgnited fire gases rise to the ceiling and spread horizontally — smoke appears to suddenly start burning. A warning sign: if not cooled/ventilated it leads to flashover.
FlashoverThe sudden, simultaneous ignition of everything in a room — the most dangerous stage. Temps soar to ~1,000°F in seconds; even a firefighter in full gear is unlikely to survive.
BackdraftOxygen-starved fire explodes when a door/window suddenly admits air. Warning signs: dark smoke pulsing in/out, no visible flame, hot door.
Stack EffectHigh-rise chimney effect: warm interior air rises through stairs/shafts, pulling smoke up. Stronger in winter, makes upper floors smoke-fill fast.
4 Ways to ExtinguishRemove fuel (isolate/burn out) · remove oxygen (smother/cover) · remove heat (cool with water) · break the chemical chain reaction (dry chemical agent).
2 · Fire Protection Systems
Wet Pipe SprinklerPipes always filled with water. Most common. Heat melts the fusible link on a head → water discharges immediately from THAT head only.
Dry Pipe SprinklerPipes filled with pressurized air; water held back by a dry-pipe valve. Used in unheated areas (parking garages, attics) to prevent freezing.
Preaction SprinklerTwo-step: a detection signal opens the valve, THEN a sprinkler head must also fuse. Used where water damage must be avoided (data centers, museums).
Deluge SystemAll heads are OPEN. When a detector trips, water floods every head simultaneously. Used in high-hazard areas (aircraft hangars, transformer rooms).
OS&Y ValveOutside Stem & Yoke — main control valve. Stem sticking OUT past the wheel = OPEN. Stem flush/IN with the wheel = CLOSED. FLSD checks this on the daily inspection.
PIVPost Indicator Valve — outdoor sprinkler control valve with an "OPEN/SHUT" window. Same function as OS&Y, different form factor.
Water Flow SwitchDetects water moving in a sprinkler pipe and trips the alarm. Confirms an actual discharge, not just an electrical signal.
Tamper SwitchDetects a sprinkler control valve being closed and sends a Supervisory signal to the FACP. Prevents a closed valve from going unnoticed.
Standpipe Class IFor FDNY use only — 2.5" hose outlets. The standard in NYC office HRBs.
Standpipe Class IIFor occupant use — 1.5" hose with hose already attached. Largely phased out; occupants should evacuate, not fight fires.
Standpipe Class IIICombination — both 2.5" (FDNY) and 1.5" (occupant) outlets at each floor.
Siamese ConnectionThe dual-inlet fitting on the building exterior where FDNY pumpers boost water pressure into the standpipe/sprinkler system. Must stay unobstructed.
Annunciator PanelDisplay showing which zone/floor/device is in alarm. Found at the FCC and sometimes at building entrances for FDNY use.
Central StationFDNY-approved off-site service that receives the building's alarm signals and re-transmits to FDNY dispatch. Also notifies the FLSD of trouble/supervisory conditions.
6 Initiating DevicesPer the manual: (a) smoke detectors (b) heat detectors (c) sprinkler water-flow devices (d) manual pull stations (e) carbon-monoxide detectors (f) flammable/combustible gas detectors.
Smoke vs Heat DetectorSmoke detectors detect most fires faster (photoelectric or ionization). Heat detectors trip on high temp or rapid temp-rise — used where smoke detectors would false alarm.
Elevator-Lobby Smoke Det.When activated, automatically recalls elevators to the designated landing (Phase I). Duct smoke detectors shut down HVAC to stop smoke spread.
Single vs Double PullSingle-action pull = 1 step (pull). Double-action = 2 steps (e.g. push-in/lift then pull) to reduce accidental activation. FLSD must visually identify each. Located on each floor near exits — conspicuous, unobstructed, accessible.
Pull Reset FirstAn activated pull station (or initiating device) must be reset BEFORE the main FACP can be reset. The FACP is only reset at the direction of an FDNY representative.
Rate-of-Rise Heat Det.Activates on a RAPID temperature increase. Does NOT require replacement after activating — it resets. More sensitive than fixed-temperature.
Fixed-Temp Heat Det.Component MELTS at a preset temperature to activate. Normally REQUIRES replacement after activating (some 'intelligent' ones reset). Most commonly used type.
Dummy Device = UnlawfulIt's unlawful to install anything shaped like fire equipment that doesn't do a fire-protection job — e.g. a hidden camera inside a fake smoke detector. It could be confused with real equipment.
Gas Detector ExceptionA flammable/combustible gas detector on the FACP gives a SUPERVISORY signal (no alarm sounds) — but FDNY IS notified to respond. Follow the plan until they arrive.
3 · FLSD Duties & Responsibilities
Call 911 FIRSTFor any fire OR non-fire emergency, 911 is called FIRST. The FLSD calls 911 and states what part of the plan is being implemented. Occupants/staff also call 911 first, then notify the FLSD.
When FLSD RequiredPresent during regular business hours AND when there are 500+ occupants, or 100+ occupants above/below street level. Can be relieved by the Deputy FLSD (both hold the F-89).
Daily FCC InspectionFDNY-recommended daily visual inspection of the Fire Command Center to detect defective components or abnormalities. Any defect found must be corrected. Industry standard practice.
Report to FCCOn a fire, alarm activation, non-fire, or medical emergency the FLSD reports to the Fire Command Center. Only an FLSD C-of-F holder may operate the FACP during business hours.
Docs at the FCCThe FDNY Plan, Floor Plans, and Building Information Card (BIC) must be kept at the Fire Command Center for responding firefighters.
Conduct Fire DrillsFLSD conducts initial + refresher training and fire drills. EXCEPTION: drills may be run by a Fire Drill Conductor (W-07) under the FLSD's supervision. FLSD also selects brigade members.
Daily Warden CheckFLSD does a daily check of the availability of FLS wardens / deputy wardens and makes notifications or temporary assignments to ensure adequate staffing.
Daily Fire-PreventionCheck exits/stairs/halls clear (aisle ≥3 ft), self-closing doors not chocked, exit signs lit, no ignition sources, no rubbish buildup, no-smoking enforced.
Don IdentificationDuring the plan or drills, all FLS staff must identify themselves to occupants by donning a vest, armband, or other identifier of their role.
3b · FLS Staff Roles (Org Chart)
FLS Director (FLSD)Holds the F-89 C of F. Runs the Fire Command Center, implements the FDNY plan, trains and supervises all FLS staff. Present during business hours.
Deputy FLSDAlso holds the F-89 C of F. Performs the FLSD's duties when the FLSD is absent (except the FLSD must personally supervise all staff training).
Bldg Evac Supervisor (BES)Office buildings only. Carries out FLSD duties after regular business hours when occupants remain. NOTE: the BES does NOT hold a C of F (unlike the FLSD/Deputy, who hold the F-89).
FLS BrigadeHotels & office buildings. On the fire floor they notify the FCC, then report to the floor below the fire to assist evacuation, control small fires, and meet firefighters. Cannot be the FLSD/Deputy.
FLS WardenOffice buildings only. ONE per floor. Notifies the FLSD via warden phone, directs floor evacuation/relocation, confirms the floor is clear by visual inspection.
Deputy FLS WardenOffice buildings. One per employer per floor, plus one for each 7,500 sq ft. Assists / covers the warden; searches all areas of the floor.
FLS SearcherOffice buildings. At least one MALE and one FEMALE per employer per floor — so all restrooms and enclosed spaces can be searched.
Critical Ops StaffOffice buildings. Personnel who stay to shut down or maintain critical operations/essential services before they shelter, relocate, or evacuate.
4 · Emergency Action Plan (EAP)
4 EAP ActionsThe four non-fire emergency actions: (1) Shelter in Place (2) In-Building Relocation (3) Partial Evacuation (4) Full Evacuation. Know all four definitions.
In-Building RelocationMove occupants to a safe location in the SAME building at least THREE floors below their present location. Not evacuation to the street. Relocating up or to the roof increases danger.
Which Floors FirstFire/alarm floor, the floor ABOVE, and the floor BELOW (if applicable) leave first → relocate ≥3 floors below. Then an all-call to the whole building.
Shelter in PlaceOccupants on floors with NO alarm activity generally stay put and don't move around the building, pending FLSD/FDNY direction. Keeps stairs clear.
Partial vs Full EvacPartial = some floors leave the building. Full = the entire building evacuates to the assembly area. Full evac is implemented only when conditions warrant.
Bomb ThreatCall 911 immediately (NYPD-led). Document the call. Do NOT use a manual pull station. Follow NYPD direction on search vs. evacuation.
Active ShooterRun / Hide / Fight. Do NOT pull the fire alarm — it can send people into corridors/stairs when they should shelter in place.
Disabled OccupantsPre-identified in the FDNY plan. Designated brigade members assist them; move to the Area of Refuge to await FDNY rescue.
Trapped & Can't ExitRetreat to a room with a solid door, call 911, give your location, and seal gaps around the door (wet towels/duct tape) against smoke.
5 · FDNY Communications
Verbal Is PrimaryThe primary method of communication during a fire emergency MUST be verbal. Text or email must not be the primary method — too slow and unreliable.
Notify OrderNotify the affected floors FIRST — fire floor, floor above, floor below (if applicable) — then make an all-call announcement to the whole building.
Warden PhonesTwo-way phones between the FCC and each floor's warden station. Usually a SINGLE line — callers must wait for the FCC to answer; calls can't all be answered at once.
The 4 W'sFor non-fire announcements: What occurred · Where it occurred · What plan provisions (T.I.P.S.) will be implemented · Why occupants must follow the actions.
Calling 911Most critical info: nature of emergency + address. Also: occupancy type, callback number, fire location, whether the FDNY plan was implemented. Don't hang up until told.
On FDNY ArrivalNotify firefighting personnel of the nature of the emergency and the response taken, and comply with their directions. Command transfers to FDNY.
6 · Building Construction & High-Rise
Fire-Rated WallWall rated to contain fire for a specified time (1-hr, 2-hr, 3-hr). Penetrations must be firestopped. Holes from cable installs = code violation.
Fire DoorSelf-closing, latching, rated door. Must NEVER be propped open. Common violation. Hold-opens must be magnetic and release on alarm.
Smokeproof TowerStairwell with a vestibule/balcony at each floor — smoke can't enter the stair without crossing open air. Used in some older HRBs.
Stair PressurizationFans pressurize the stairwell to keep smoke out. Activated automatically on alarm. Modern HRB equivalent of smokeproof tower.
Area of RefugeProtected location (rated stair landing or rated room) where disabled occupants wait for FDNY rescue. Usually has a two-way comm device.
Fire Command CenterPrincipal location where alarm/communication/control status is displayed and managed. Located in the LOBBY on the main entrance floor near the FDNY response point.
Fire TowerA stairway with a vestibule/balcony giving extra smoke protection. Given PRIORITY for occupant evacuation/relocation when present.
Fail-Safe DeviceElectro-magnetic lock holding a stairway/corridor door. Note: a manual pull alarm may NOT release them automatically — the FLSD must manually release after confirming fire/smoke.
Stairway ReentryLocked stairway doors typically allow reentry every fourth floor and should unlock on alarm or power loss. Some pre-1968 buildings lock all but the street level.
Class J / Class EOlder voice-comm building classes: Class-J (now R-1, hotels) and Class-E (now Group B, offices). 2008 code requires voice comm for most high-rises.
Vertical SpreadFire/smoke spreads UP through shafts, stairs, plenums, poke-throughs. Why floor-above evacuation is critical.
6b · Two-Way Communication Systems
Warden PhonesUsed by BUILDING OCCUPANTS. Located near exit stairways on each floor; also installed in the FACP. Must communicate with the Fire Command Center. Used to issue instructions during a fire or non-fire emergency.
FCC Can't Call FloorsThe FCC cannot call individual warden phones. If the FLSD needs a floor, the FLSD makes an announcement instructing that floor warden to initiate contact (call in).
ARC SystemAuxiliary Radio Communication — used by FIREFIGHTERS, POLICE & emergency services. A wireless two-way system that receives & transmits FDNY portable radio frequencies inside the building.
ARC Out of ServiceThe FDNY must be notified IMMEDIATELY if the ARC System is out of service, and an Out-of-Service tag placed at the Fire Command Center (or other approved location).
6c · Alarm-Activated Building Features
Fire / Smoke DampersOpen and close to provide fresh air or to stop fire/smoke passage through ductwork.
Hold-Open DeviceMagnetic release that lets self-closing doors close automatically upon activation of the building's fire alarm system.
Elevator RecallRecalls elevators to the designated landing (usually the lobby) in the event of an alarm — triggered by elevator-lobby smoke detectors.
Fail-Safe Door ReleaseUnlocks stairway/exit doors from the STAIR side so occupants can re-enter a floor. A manual pull may NOT release them automatically — FLSD may release manually after confirming fire/smoke.
HVAC Auto ShutdownDuct smoke detectors shut down the HVAC to stop smoke spreading. The FLSD may need to consult the building engineer for assistance.
7 · NYC Fire Code & Regulations
Occupancy: BBusiness — offices. The occupancy class F-89 covers. Daytime population, low fuel load, alert adult occupants.
Occupancy: AAssembly — 50+ people gathered (auditoriums, restaurants, conference centers). Higher exit and sprinkler requirements.
Occupancy: RResidential — hotels (R-1), apartments (R-2). Different CoF (T-89/F-89 hotel) applies; sleeping occupants change the strategy.
Office Sprinkler RuleBy 07/01/2019, all NYC office buildings 100 ft or more in height had to be fully sprinklered throughout. New Group A and R-1 (hotel) buildings require sprinklers under the 2008 code.
Exit Aisle WidthAn exit aisle is generally required to be at least 3 feet wide and kept clear. Locks/bolts/chains must not be on exit doors while the building is in use.
Unwarranted Alarms1 is allowable. 2 or more in any 3-month period is unlawful: 1st = warning, 2nd = ticket (summons). Examples: testing without taking it offline, construction dust, shower steam, cigarette smoke.
Smoke Detector CleaningWhen a smoke detector reports the need for maintenance to the FACP, it must be cleaned within 1 week (NFPA 72).
Record RetentionStandpipe/sprinkler inspection, test, and maintenance records kept on premises a minimum of 3 years (FC 901.6.2 / NFPA 25).
Comprehensive PlanEvery office HRB must have an FDNY-accepted Comprehensive Fire Safety & Emergency Action (Level 1) Plan. The FLSD implements it.
8 · FLSD First Actions on a Fire Alarm
0 · 911 FirstEnsure 911 is called FIRST for any fire/non-fire emergency and state that the FDNY plan is being implemented. This overrides everything — even if auto-transmission fired.
1 · Report to FCCImmediately report to the Fire Command Center (or the designated alternate if the FCC is compromised).
2 · AcknowledgeAcknowledge the alarm at the FACP (if applicable). This may identify the device or zone in alarm.
3 · Address the PanelVerify the location and which initiating device activated: pull station · smoke/beam/duct detector · heat detector · water-flow device.
4 · Recall ElevatorsEnsure all elevators are recalled (Phase I) so they don't carry occupants to the fire floor or trap them.
5 · Ensure 911 CalledConfirm 911 has been called — never rely solely on automatic transmission. State that the FDNY plan is being implemented.
6 · Communicate w/ StaffContact FLS wardens on the alarm floor(s) / direct brigade members to learn: fire location, severity, floors & stairways affected by smoke, occupant count.
7 · Identify StairsPick a stairway for occupant evacuation (fire tower first) and a separate one for firefighters. Ensure all fail-safe devices are released so doors open.
8 · AnnounceNotify the affected floors first (fire floor, above, below), instruct relocation ≥3 floors below via the designated stair, no elevators. Then all-call.
9 · Shelter UnaffectedInstruct floors with no alarm activity to shelter in place and not move around the building, pending further direction.
10 · MonitorMonitor the FACP for fire progression and maintain regular communication with FLS staff until FDNY relieves you.
9 · Fire Command Center Operations
Alarm SignalA fire-initiating device activated (pull station, smoke/heat detector, water-flow). FACP sounds horns/strobes, signals the central station, and controls building functions. FDNY IS dispatched.
Supervisory SignalA fire-protection system is compromised — e.g. closed control valve (tamper switch), low air/water, gravity-tank temp/level, pump trouble. FLSD investigates & corrects. FDNY NOT dispatched — EXCEPTION: a flammable/combustible gas detector on the FACP gives a supervisory signal but FDNY IS notified.
Trouble SignalAn electrical fault in the alarm system itself — battery, AC failure, ground fault, open/short circuit, phone-line failure. FLSD investigates & calls the contractor. FDNY is NOT dispatched.
Acknowledge (ACK)Acknowledges alarm/trouble/supervisory conditions; may identify the device or zone. Does NOT clear the condition.
Alarm SilenceSilences building horns/strobes after evacuation while the source is investigated. Does NOT stop the signal going to the central station. Never silence until FDNY verifies.
System ResetResets the panel after the condition is cleared. Won't reset to normal while an initiating device is still in alarm. Never reset until FDNY verifies. Fans usually need a separate restart.
Never Silence/ResetNEVER silence or reset the fire alarm until the condition is verified by FDNY firefighting personnel.
Lamp TestLights up all panel LEDs to confirm they work. Part of the recommended daily visual inspection.
10 · Human Behavior in Emergencies
Denial PhaseMost occupants' first reaction is to disbelieve the alarm. They finish emails, gather belongings, hesitate. Clear, calm announcements override denial.
Crowd FlowPeople follow the leader and the crowd — not the signs. Trained Fire Wardens visibly directing the flow are more effective than any sign.
BottlenecksForm at stair doors, lobby exits, and turns. Fire Wardens keep people moving, prevent stopping to look back, manage pace.
Panic Is RareTrue panic is uncommon — most people are slow, not panicked. The real problem is delayed start, not chaos. Speed up the start.
Calm VoiceFLSD announcements should be slow, clear, low-pitch, repeated. Urgency in the words; calm in the delivery. Panicked voice creates panic.
Familiar RoutesPeople instinctively return to the way they came in — even if it's blocked. Drills train them to use the nearest exit instead.
Extinguisher LimitsPortable extinguishers stop a SMALL fire from growing — they are NOT intended to fight large or spreading fires. If the door's hot or smoke is visible, don't enter; evacuate.
Fire Doubles / MinuteIn a non-sprinklered building, a fire can double in size every minute — why immediate response to an alarm is critical and hesitation is deadly.
